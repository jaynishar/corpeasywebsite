FROM SHARED DESKS TO STRATEGIC COMMAND CENTRES: WHY INDIA'S CORPORATE BOOM IS MAKING MANAGED OFFICES THE NEW STANDARD

As GCCs and MNCs outgrow the co-working model, CorpEasy is redefining how businesses occupy space in Mumbai.

MUMBAI, India — April 2026 — The co-working space was never meant for a 500-person team.

When Brad Neuberg opened the world's first co-working space in San Francisco in 2005, the concept was elegantly simple: give freelancers and small startups a professional place to work. The model was built around one person, or a team of five to ten.

That vision powered one of the most explosive workplace revolutions in modern history. By 2014, there were nearly 6,000 co-working spaces worldwide. In India, flexible workspace found its most fertile ground.

But something fundamental has changed.

THE PROBLEM WITH CO-WORKING AT SCALE

Co-working was designed for agility at the individual level. Hot desks. Month-to-month memberships. Shared everything. For a solo developer or a two-founder startup, this was a revelation.

For a Global Capability Centre (GCC) deploying 300 engineers, or an MNC setting up a regional headquarters in Mumbai, it is a logistical mismatch.

India is now the undisputed GCC capital of the world. As of 2025, the country hosts over 1,760 GCCs employing nearly 2 million professionals and generating USD 64.6 billion in annual revenue. In 2025 alone, GCCs accounted for 38% of all office leasing across India's top seven cities — a record 31.3 million square feet. By 2030, over 2,500 GCCs are projected to operate across the country.

These are not five-person startups looking for a hot desk. These are companies like Google, Goldman Sachs, and Bosch — running full-scale innovation centres, managing global mandates, and building products that span multiple time zones. Their Mumbai offices are strategic assets, not satellite spaces.

And a standard co-working membership cannot serve that reality.

The pain points are well-documented among enterprise occupiers: lack of brand identity in shared environments, security and data privacy concerns in open-plan floors, inability to customise space for specialised functions, management overhead when facilities are nobody's full-time responsibility, and lease structures that do not align with enterprise planning cycles.

THE MANAGED OFFICE: FLEXIBILITY MEETS ENTERPRISE GRADE

The managed office is what co-working always pointed toward — but could never fully become.

Unlike a traditional lease, a managed office is fully fitted, fully serviced, and operationally ready. Unlike a standard co-working desk, it is an exclusive, branded environment purpose-built for one company. The organisation gets its own dedicated space — its own identity, access controls, and IT infrastructure — without the capital burden of fit-out, facility management, or long-term lease commitments.

India's co-working market registered a record 12.5 million square feet of leased managed workspace in 2024 — a 44% year-over-year increase — driven almost entirely by enterprise and GCC demand. The data tells a clear story: the future of flexible work in India belongs to the managed model.

"Businesses don't need desks. They need environments where their teams can do their best work," said Dev Doshi, Co-Founder of CorpEasy. "A managed office gives you everything a traditional lease offers — identity, security, customisation — without the 10-month build-out, the vendor management headaches, or the capital locked into furniture and IT infrastructure. You walk in on day one and your team is productive."
